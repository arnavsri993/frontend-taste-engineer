# Comparing frontend design directions

Use direction comparison to make taste inspectable without pretending that a weighted score can choose a design. The rubric narrows disagreement, exposes risk, and records why a direction serves the product. A non-negotiable failure always outranks the total.

## Prepare comparable candidates

Use the same outcome-first brief, content outline, difficult real-data fixtures, viewport set, and required states for every candidate. A candidate should be a coherent direction, not a different colorway. Vary meaningful axes such as dense versus spacious, quiet versus expressive, editorial versus instrumental, familiar versus novel, and image-led versus type-led.

Each candidate should name:

- its one-sentence intent;
- its target position on the relevant axes;
- two or three signature signals;
- what remains deliberately familiar;
- the hardest content, state, and responsive condition it was tested against;
- assumptions and evidence gaps.

## Compare decisions, not screenshots

For every rubric criterion, record a score, confidence, and one sentence of evidence. Low-confidence high scores are prompts for further testing, not advantages. Review the candidates in these modes:

1. Text outline: validates task and content hierarchy.
2. Grayscale: exposes hierarchy that depends on hue or effects.
3. Narrow, transition, and wide widths: exposes composition fragility.
4. Long content, pseudo-localization, error, empty, and loading fixtures: exposes template dependence.
5. Keyboard, large text, forced colors, and reduced motion: exposes directions that require inaccessible styling.
6. Production-budget estimate: exposes directions whose identity depends on excessive media, fonts, effects, or client code.

## Use anti-pattern detectors carefully

An anti-pattern record is a diagnostic, not a universal ban. A detected card grid, pill family, gradient, bento layout, oversized hero, layered effect, or reveal animation must either have a contextual rationale and pass its verification checks or be replaced by the listed alternatives. Recording a false positive is preferable to disabling the detector globally.

## Make the decision

Select a direction only when it passes all non-negotiable criteria and its strongest qualities are systemic. If no candidate passes, revise the brief or candidates; do not average them into a compromise. Combining parts is allowed only when the selected decisions are named, compatible, and re-evaluated as one coherent system.

The decision record should state what was selected, what was rejected, the decisive evidence, known risks, assumptions to validate, and the conditions that would trigger reconsideration. Keep the record with the implementation so later changes can distinguish purposeful evolution from drift.
